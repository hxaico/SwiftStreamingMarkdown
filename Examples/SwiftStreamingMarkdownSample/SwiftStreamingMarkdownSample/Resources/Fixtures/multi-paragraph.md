# Multi-paragraph Demonstration

## *Pride and Prejudice* (Jane Austen, 1813)

It is a truth universally acknowledged, that a single man in possession of a good fortune, must be in want of a wife.

However little known the feelings or views of such a man may be on his first entering a neighbourhood, this truth is so well fixed in the minds of the surrounding families, that he is considered as the rightful property of some one or other of their daughters.

When Mr. Bingley first arrives at Netherfield, the Bennet household is instantly animated by speculation, hope, and anxiety. The drawing-room conversation grows louder, each sister reflecting a different temperament: playful, practical, romantic, or reserved.

Elizabeth watches this social theatre with intelligence and irony. Her wit sharpens in proportion to the absurdity she observes, and her judgments, though keen, are never entirely free from her own pride.

## *Moby-Dick* (Herman Melville, 1851)

Call me Ishmael. Some years ago—never mind how long precisely—having little or no money in my purse, and nothing particular to interest me on shore, I thought I would sail about a little and see the watery part of the world.

It is a way I have of driving off the spleen and regulating the circulation.

The sea, in Melville's telling, is not merely geography but temperament: restless, vast, and indifferent. Ships become floating societies, and every rope, mast, and watch-bell carries a philosophy of labor and fate.

As the Pequod sails farther from ordinary life, Captain Ahab's private obsession transforms into the crew's collective destiny. The voyage becomes less a hunt and more a confrontation with the limits of will.

## *A Tale of Two Cities* (Charles Dickens, 1859)

It was the best of times, it was the worst of times, it was the age of wisdom, it was the age of foolishness, it was the epoch of belief, it was the epoch of incredulity.

It was the season of Light, it was the season of Darkness, it was the spring of hope, it was the winter of despair.

Between London and Paris, Dickens presents two cities bound by hidden debts, old injuries, and sudden acts of grace. In crowded streets and quiet chambers alike, private choices ripple into public consequence.

The novel moves between tenderness and terror, insisting that human beings are capable of cruelty and sacrifice in equal measure. Its final pages transform political violence into a meditation on redemption.

## *The Great Gatsby* (F. Scott Fitzgerald, 1925)

In my younger and more vulnerable years my father gave me some advice that I've been turning over in my mind ever since. "Whenever you feel like criticising any one," he told me, "just remember that all the people in this world haven't had the advantages that you've had."

Across summer evenings on Long Island, wealth appears as performance: lights, music, and rumor drifting over water. Yet beneath the glitter lies loneliness, misrecognition, and the stubborn force of the past.

Nick's narration watches dreams harden into symbols, and symbols dissolve into tragedy. The green light, the valley of ashes, and the ceaseless parties all point to a single tension between reinvention and reality.
